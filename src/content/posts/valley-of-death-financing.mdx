I spent my career in markets — pricing risk, structuring capital, figuring out who actually gets paid and when. I'm now pointing that at defense tech, and the first thing that jumped out is a number:

Only about **5% of SBIR Phase II companies ever cross into a Phase III / Program of Record.** The other 95% built something the government said it wanted — and then died in the gap before a real contract.

The reflex is to call that a technology problem. It isn't. Look at the mechanics:

- STRATFI and TACFI — the bridges across the gap — don't just hand you money. STRATFI runs $3M–$15M and TACFI $375K–$2M, and both require **matching capital** (dual-use runs 1:1:2 — SBIR dollars : government : private). So crossing the valley isn't an engineering milestone. It's a **capital-structuring** milestone.
- The companies that make it aren't always the best tech. They're the ones who lined up the match, managed working capital through 6–12 month payment lags, and didn't run out of cash waiting on a slow customer.
- Today that's solved by *consultants* (Long Capture, Elara Nova) charging billable hours, and *legacy lenders* (Parabilis, Leonid, Republic Capital Access) doing factoring at factoring prices. There's no modern, software-native version — no "Ramp for GovCon."

In any other industry, "the best products die because of capital timing, and the only fixes are expensive consultants and factoring" is a sentence that ends with a fintech getting built.

I don't have the answer yet — I have the question, and a finance brain that finds this stuff obvious. So I'm going to spend the next few weeks talking to SBIR founders who crossed the valley (or didn't) and the investors who matched them, and I'll write up what I learn here, in public.
